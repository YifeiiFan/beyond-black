4. Psychological Structure: Turning Inner Monologue into Prayer

Psychologically, one of the most important differences between everyday loneliness and religious solitude lies in what happens to inner speech.

In many lonely states, inner speech turns against the self. The voice in the head is either blank or hostile: there is nothing to say, or there is only accusation. The person replays perceived mistakes, awkward moments, and imagined judgments. Being alone with oneself feels cramped and unpleasant. It makes sense to reach for noise and distraction.

In religious solitude, inner speech is deliberately re-shaped. Instead of a free-floating monologue, it is framed as prayer, meditation, or reflection “before God.” The content may be gratitude, confession, complaint, or simple sharing, but structurally the speech has a form: “You… I…”

Thomas Merton, writing from monastic life, insists that real solitude is not a flight from relationship but a different way of being in it. The solitary is most alone, he says, when they are most exposed to the gaze of God; in that gaze, fear of being seen and the desire to be seen are both drawn out (Merton). Solitude becomes a place where the inner life is allowed to appear, not something that must be covered over.

This reorganisation of inner speech matters in at least two ways.

First, it keeps inner dialogue from collapsing. Even when God feels absent, the act of addressing presupposes a listener. This resists the slide into Arendt’s deserted self, in which one no longer feels accompanied even by one’s own thoughts (Arendt).

Second, it borrows the language of tradition. Religious communities provide psalms, prayers, and narratives that can be taken up as one’s own words. Someone who feels abandoned can pray, “Why have you forsaken me?” and know that this sentence is not merely their private complaint but part of a long, shared repertoire. Research on “positive solitude” suggests that when people have meaningful frameworks for their time alone—creative, reflective, or spiritual—they are more likely to experience solitude as peaceful rather than distressing (Shiovitz-Ezra and Rozen).

In this sense, the psychological depth of religious solitude lies in the fact that loneliness is not simply suppressed or turned into self-blame. It is given a form of speech, addressed to Someone, in continuity with the words of others.
